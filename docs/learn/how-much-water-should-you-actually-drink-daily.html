How much water should you actually drink daily?

The Short Answer Most People Get Wrong

Eight glasses a day. You've heard it a thousand times. It's also not quite right — or rather, it's not wrong for everyone, but it's not a universal truth either. The actual recommendation from the U.S. National Academies of Sciences is about 3.7 litres (125 oz) for men and 2.7 litres (91 oz) for women per day in total fluid intake. Note the word total: that includes water from food, coffee, tea, juice, and everything else you drink.

When you strip out the water you get from food (roughly 20% of daily intake for most people eating a normal diet), you land at around 2.5–3 litres of fluid from drinks for men and 2–2.5 litres for women. Eight 8-ounce glasses is about 1.9 litres. Close, but consistently a bit low, and that's before you factor in heat, exercise, or body size.

Why "One Number Fits All" Falls Apart

Your kidneys can process somewhere between 0.8 and 1 litre of water per hour. Drink way above your needs and you dilute electrolytes; drink chronically below them and you carry a mild hydration deficit that affects concentration, physical performance, and digestion. But your needs shift constantly based on:

  • Body weight and composition. A 90 kg person needs more water than a 55 kg person for baseline kidney function and cellular hydration alone.
  • Activity level. You can lose 0.5–2 litres per hour of exercise through sweat, more in heat or at altitude.
  • Climate. Living somewhere hot and humid means you sweat passively all day even sitting still.
  • Diet. A diet heavy in fruits, vegetables, and soups contributes significantly to hydration. A dry, high-sodium diet increases your need.
  • Health status. Kidney conditions, diabetes, heart failure, and certain medications all change the equation.
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding. The recommendation rises to roughly 3 litres per day during breastfeeding.

A More Practical Formula

If you want a number that actually accounts for your body, a widely used clinical starting point is:

30–35 ml of water per kilogram of body weight per day.

So a 70 kg person needs roughly 2,100–2,450 ml (2.1–2.45 litres) from all fluid sources. A 90 kg person is looking at 2,700–3,150 ml. Add roughly 500–1,000 ml for each hour of moderate exercise, and another 500 ml if you're in a hot environment or running a fever.

This formula isn't perfect — nothing is — but it's far more personalised than a flat eight-glass rule and it's what many registered dietitians use as a baseline before adjusting.

Reading Your Body's Signals

Numbers aside, your body gives you reliable real-time feedback if you pay attention.

Urine colour

The most practical indicator most people have access to at any moment. Aim for pale straw yellow — the colour of weak lemonade. Dark amber or brown means you're behind on fluids. Completely colourless can mean you've been overdoing it or, more likely, you took a B-vitamin that morning.

Keep in mind that certain foods (beetroot, asparagus) and medications can alter colour regardless of hydration status, and your first morning urine is always more concentrated because you haven't drunk anything overnight.

Thirst

Thirst is real and worth responding to, but it's a lagging indicator. By the time you feel noticeably thirsty, you're already mildly dehydrated — roughly 1–2% below optimal body water. That sounds small, but research consistently shows cognitive performance and physical endurance both decline at that level. Thirst is a good signal to act on; it's not a reliable system for prevention.

Other signs you may be under-hydrated

  • Headaches, particularly in the afternoon
  • Difficulty concentrating or brain fog mid-morning
  • Constipation or infrequent urination (fewer than 3–4 times a day)
  • Dry mouth despite not being in a dry environment
  • Muscle cramps during or after exercise

Common Situations That Change Your Needs

Exercise

For workouts under an hour at moderate intensity, matching your sweat loss with water is fine. For longer or harder sessions, you'll also need to replace electrolytes — particularly sodium — otherwise you can develop hyponatremia (dangerously low blood sodium) from drinking too much plain water. Sports drinks, electrolyte tablets, or simply salting your food before a long event solves this.

A practical approach: weigh yourself before and after exercise. Each kilogram lost is approximately 1 litre of fluid deficit. Drink 1.5 litres for every kilogram lost to account for ongoing losses.

Hot weather or travel

Flying dehydrates you faster than most people realise — cabin humidity sits around 10–20%, far drier than most climates. Drink an extra glass before boarding and avoid relying on in-flight service alone. In heat waves, baseline needs can rise by 0.5–1 litre per day even without deliberate exercise.

Illness

Fever, vomiting, and diarrhoea all accelerate fluid loss dramatically. The standard advice of "clear fluids" is correct, but plain water isn't always enough — you lose electrolytes too. Oral rehydration solutions (or a homemade equivalent of water, salt, and sugar) are more effective than water alone for illness-related dehydration.

Older adults

The sensation of thirst diminishes with age, meaning older people can become chronically dehydrated without feeling it. If you're over 65, building in deliberate drinking habits — a glass with every meal, for example — is more reliable than waiting for thirst signals.

Does Coffee and Tea Count?

Yes. The diuretic effect of caffeine at typical consumption levels (1–4 cups per day) is mild and does not cause net fluid loss in regular caffeine drinkers whose bodies have adapted. The fluid in your coffee counts toward your daily total. Same for tea, sparkling water, milk, and juice — though juice comes with sugar, and alcohol genuinely does cause net dehydration.

Pitfalls to Avoid

Gulping large amounts at once. Spreading intake across the day is more effective than drinking a litre in one sitting. Your kidneys process fluid gradually; flooding the system means a lot of it exits quickly.

Counting only plain water. Unnecessarily restrictive and usually inaccurate — it leads people to both under-count what they're getting and over-drink plain water on top.

Ignoring sodium. Hydration isn't just about volume; it's about the balance of water and electrolytes inside and outside cells. If you're drinking large amounts of water but eating very low sodium, you may still feel sluggish because of electrolyte imbalance.

Obsessing over daily totals. Day-to-day variation is normal. One day of lower intake won't harm you; you're aiming for a consistent average across the week.

A Simple Daily Framework

If you want practical habits without constantly doing mental arithmetic:

1. Start the day with a large glass of water before coffee — you've gone 7–8 hours without drinking anything. 2. Drink a glass with every meal — that's roughly 600 ml covered without thinking about it. 3. Carry a water bottle and aim to finish it once or twice depending on size — a 750 ml bottle finished twice is 1.5 litres just from that. 4. Drink before, during, and after any exercise, not just during. 5. Check your urine colour once or twice a day and adjust if it's dark.

That framework lands most average-sized adults in the right range without obsessive tracking.

FAQ

Can you drink too much water? Yes, though it's uncommon in healthy people going about their normal day. Overhydration matters most in endurance athletes who drink large volumes of plain water over several hours. For everyday life, your kidneys handle variation well.

Does water intake affect weight loss? Drinking water before meals reduces calorie intake slightly in some studies. Staying well-hydrated also reduces water retention, which affects scale weight. But water isn't a fat-loss tool on its own — the mechanisms are modest.

Is sparkling water as hydrating as still water? Yes. The carbonation doesn't affect how water is absorbed. Some people find sparkling water easier to drink in quantity.

Why do I still feel thirsty after drinking a lot of water? Persistent thirst despite adequate intake can indicate elevated blood sugar, high sodium intake, certain medications, or occasionally anxiety. If it's ongoing, it's worth mentioning to a doctor.

Does the "8 glasses" rule have any scientific origin? It loosely traces back to a 1945 U.S. Food and Nutrition Board recommendation that suggested 2.5 litres per day — but the same document noted that most of this came from food. The "from food" part got dropped somewhere along the way, and the eight-glasses rule took on a life of its own.
