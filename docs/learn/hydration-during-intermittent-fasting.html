Hydration during intermittent fasting

Why Hydration Matters More When You're Fasting

When you eat regularly, food contributes roughly 20–30% of your daily fluid intake. Fruits, vegetables, soups, and even cooked grains carry significant water. Cut out meals for 16, 18, or 24 hours and that source disappears entirely. Your body still loses fluid through breathing, sweating, and urination at the same rate — it just isn't getting the passive top-up that food normally provides.

There's a second factor worth understanding: insulin. During a fast, insulin levels drop, which signals the kidneys to excrete more sodium. Water follows sodium out of the body. This is part of why people drop several pounds in the first few days of fasting — much of it is water and glycogen-bound fluid, not fat. The practical consequence is that you can become mildly dehydrated faster than you'd expect, even if you're drinking the same amount you always have.

How Much Water Should You Actually Drink?

The "eight glasses a day" rule was always a rough estimate, and it's particularly imprecise during a fast. A more useful starting point is 35 ml per kilogram of body weight, adjusted upward if you're active or live somewhere hot. For a 70 kg person, that's roughly 2.4 litres from beverages alone — more than most people drink on an eating day, let alone a fasting one.

Pay attention to urine colour. Pale straw yellow is the target. Dark amber means you're behind. Completely clear can mean you're diluting your electrolytes, which creates its own problems (more on that below).

Spread your intake across the day rather than drinking large amounts in one sitting. The kidneys can process about 800 ml to 1 litre per hour. Drinking a litre of water first thing in the morning and then neglecting fluids until evening is not the same as steady sipping throughout the day.

What You Can Drink Without Breaking a Fast

This depends on your goals. Metabolic fasting (lowering insulin, burning fat) has different rules than religious fasting, and both differ from time-restricted eating done primarily for weight management. Here's a practical breakdown:

Water

Plain still or sparkling water has zero calories and will not affect insulin or autophagy. This is your primary tool. Sparkling water is fine if it doesn't cause bloating that makes you miserable.

Black Coffee

A standard black coffee contains roughly 2–5 calories and has minimal impact on insulin. Most fasting protocols accept it. Caffeine is also a mild diuretic, so if you're drinking three or four cups during your fasting window, account for that by drinking extra water alongside them.

Plain Tea

Herbal teas, green tea, black tea — all fine. Avoid anything with added sugars, honey, or milk if you want to keep the fast intact. Green tea in particular contains compounds that may support fat oxidation during a fast, though the effect size is modest.

Electrolyte Drinks

Zero-calorie electrolyte mixes or electrolyte tablets dissolved in water are accepted by most serious fasters. They don't spike insulin in meaningful amounts, and they replace the sodium, magnesium, and potassium lost through increased urination. If you're doing extended fasts (20 hours or more) or exercising during your fasting window, these become genuinely important rather than optional.

What to Avoid

  • Anything with sugar or fruit juice
  • Milk or creamers in significant amounts
  • "Zero calorie" drinks sweetened with certain sugar alcohols that can still provoke an insulin response in sensitive individuals
  • Bone broth (calorie-containing, breaks a strict fast — though some protocols deliberately allow it for longer fasts)

The Electrolyte Problem Most People Overlook

Dehydration during a fast is rarely just about water. The bigger issue is electrolyte depletion, and it's the direct cause of most fasting side effects that people attribute to "hunger" or "detox."

Sodium is the most critical. When insulin drops, kidneys dump sodium. Without enough sodium, you get headaches, brain fog, low energy, and muscle cramps — the collection of symptoms often called "keto flu" or fasting fatigue. During eating windows, adding a generous pinch of sea salt to food is often sufficient. During the fasting window itself, dissolving a small amount of salt in water (a quarter teaspoon in 500 ml) is a practical fix that doesn't break a fast.

Magnesium is the next priority. It's involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions and is commonly depleted even in people who aren't fasting. Deficiency presents as muscle cramps, poor sleep, and irritability. A supplement taken during your eating window — magnesium glycinate or malate are well-tolerated forms — handles this cleanly.

Potassium can be addressed through food during eating windows. Avocados, leafy greens, salmon, and sweet potatoes are all high in potassium and fit comfortably into most fasting-adjacent eating patterns.

Practical Timing: Hydration Through a 16:8 Day

Here's how someone fasting from 8pm to 12pm the following day might structure their fluids:

7:00 am — 500 ml water on waking, possibly with a small pinch of salt or an electrolyte tablet. This replaces what you lost overnight through respiration.

8:00–9:00 am — Black coffee or plain tea. Drink another 300–400 ml of water alongside it to offset the mild diuretic effect.

10:00 am — Another 500 ml of plain water. This is when many people feel hunger most acutely; adequate hydration genuinely reduces that sensation because thirst and hunger signals can overlap.

11:30 am — Final 300 ml before the eating window opens at noon.

Noon onward (eating window) — Continue drinking water with meals. Your food will also contribute fluid. This is when to take magnesium if you use it, and when to eat potassium-rich foods.

This isn't rigid prescription — it's a template. Adjust for your schedule, your body weight, your activity level, and the weather.

Signs You're Getting It Wrong

Underhydration

  • Persistent headache that starts mid-morning
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Urine that's dark yellow or brownish
  • Dizziness when standing up quickly
  • Constipation (water is essential to bowel motility)

Overhydration / Electrolyte Dilution

Less common but worth knowing about, especially if you're drinking very large volumes in response to fasting symptoms without replacing electrolytes. Signs include nausea, headache, confusion, and in severe cases, hyponatremia (dangerously low blood sodium). Drinking three or more litres of plain water without any sodium intake during extended fasting periods puts you at risk. This is rare but not theoretical.

The fix for both problems converges on the same answer: drink adequate fluid and include electrolytes, particularly sodium.

Exercising During a Fasted State

Training in the fasted window is common — many people prefer it. The hydration rules shift slightly:

  • Drink 400–600 ml of water in the hour before training
  • If the session is under 60 minutes and low-to-moderate intensity, plain water during exercise is sufficient
  • For sessions over 60 minutes, or anything high-intensity in heat, use an electrolyte drink during the workout
  • Rehydrate promptly after: at minimum 500 ml, ideally with sodium

Performance may dip slightly during fasted exercise, especially before adaptation. Dehydration on top of that is unnecessary. Don't train fasted and underhydrated and then conclude that fasted training doesn't work for you.

FAQ

Does drinking water break a fast? No. Plain water has no calories and does not affect insulin, blood glucose, or autophagy. It is the one thing you can and should consume freely during any fasting protocol.

Can I drink sparkling water while fasting? Yes. Carbonated water is still water. Some people find it reduces hunger. If it causes significant bloating, switch to still water.

Does coffee count toward my fluid intake? Broadly yes, despite the mild diuretic effect. The net effect of moderate coffee consumption is still hydrating. Just don't rely on it exclusively.

Why do I get headaches when I fast? Usually sodium depletion or general dehydration. Try adding a small amount of salt to your water before concluding that fasting doesn't suit you.

How do I know if I'm drinking enough? Urine colour is your most reliable real-time indicator. Pale yellow, not clear and not dark. That's the range to aim for throughout the day.

Is it okay to drink electrolyte drinks during a fast? Yes, provided they contain no sugar or significant calories. Zero-calorie electrolyte formulas are widely used by people doing extended fasts and are generally considered compatible with the metabolic goals of fasting.
