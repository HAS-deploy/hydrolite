hyponatremia — hydration glossary

What it means

Hyponatremia is a condition in which the sodium concentration in the blood falls below the normal range (generally under 135 mmol/L). Sodium is the primary electrolyte controlling fluid balance outside cells, and when it drops too low, water shifts into cells causing them to swell. The brain is especially sensitive to this swelling, which is why severe hyponatremia can produce neurological symptoms ranging from nausea and headache to confusion, seizures, and in extreme cases, death.

The condition can develop in two broad ways: either the body loses sodium (through heavy sweating, vomiting, or diuretic use) or — more commonly in active people — sodium becomes diluted because too much water is consumed without adequate sodium replacement.

Why it matters

Hyponatremia comes up most often in endurance sports and medical settings. Marathoners, ultrarunners, and triathletes who drink large amounts of plain water over many hours can dilute their blood sodium faster than they lose it, leading to exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH). Slower athletes who spend more time on course and aggressively over-drink are at higher risk than faster competitors. Symptoms during or after a race — bloating, puffiness, confusion, or unusual fatigue — should raise suspicion. Hospitalizations and deaths from EAH have occurred when symptomatic athletes were mistakenly given more IV fluids, worsening the dilution.

Outside sport, hyponatremia also appears in elderly patients on diuretics, people with certain kidney or heart conditions, and psychiatric patients with compulsive water drinking.

Example

A runner completes a six-hour trail race in moderate heat. She drinks water at every aid station but skips the electrolyte drinks. Near the finish she feels nauseated and disoriented. At the medical tent her blood sodium is measured at 128 mmol/L. The treatment is sodium replacement — not more fluids — and she is monitored for several hours before being cleared.

Common confusions

Hyponatremia vs. dehydration: These are frequently conflated but are nearly opposite problems in endurance contexts. Dehydration means insufficient total fluid; hyponatremia from over-drinking means too much fluid relative to sodium. Treating hyponatremia with extra water makes it worse.

Low sodium intake vs. low blood sodium: Eating a low-sodium diet does not directly cause hyponatremia in healthy people with normal kidneys. Blood sodium is tightly regulated, and it takes significant fluid excess or unusual medical circumstances to push it into dangerous territory.

**Hyponatremia vs. hypokalemia:** Both start with "hypo" and involve electrolytes, but hypokalemia refers specifically to low potassium, not sodium. They have different causes, symptoms, and treatments.
