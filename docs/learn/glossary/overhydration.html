overhydration — hydration glossary

What it means

Overhydration is the state of having more water in the body than the body can effectively manage or excrete. It occurs when fluid intake exceeds the kidneys' ability to eliminate the excess, or when a medical condition impairs normal fluid regulation. The result is a dilution of electrolytes — particularly sodium — in the blood, a condition called hyponatremia. Mild cases may cause bloating and discomfort, while severe cases can lead to nausea, confusion, seizures, or in rare circumstances, death.

Overhydration is less common than dehydration but is not rare. It happens in endurance athletes who drink too much plain water during long events, in people following misguided advice to drink enormous volumes daily, and in patients with conditions like heart failure, kidney disease, or SIADH (syndrome of inappropriate antidiuretic hormone secretion) that cause the body to retain fluid abnormally.

Why it matters

Understanding overhydration is important because the popular message to "drink more water" can be taken too far. During marathons, ultramarathons, and triathlons, athletes who drink beyond thirst — especially plain water without electrolytes — are at genuine risk of exercise-associated hyponatremia. Symptoms like nausea and swelling can be mistaken for dehydration, leading to the dangerous mistake of drinking even more. Clinically, overhydration also matters in hospital settings, where IV fluid management requires careful balance, and in patients with impaired kidney or heart function who cannot shed excess fluid normally.

Example

A runner completes a four-hour marathon on a cool day and drinks roughly 800 ml of plain water every hour, far exceeding her sweat losses. By the finish line she feels nauseated, her hands look puffy, and she's confused. Her blood sodium has dropped to a dangerously low level. This is exercise-associated hyponatremia — a direct result of overhydration with electrolyte-free fluid.

Common confusions

Overhydration vs. water retention (edema): Edema is swelling caused by fluid accumulating in tissues, often from heart failure, venous insufficiency, or low albumin. You can have edema without drinking too much water. Overhydration specifically refers to excess total body water intake or retention affecting the blood's electrolyte concentration.

Overhydration vs. hyponatremia: Hyponatremia (low blood sodium) is the consequence of overhydration, not a synonym for it. Hyponatremia can also arise from sodium loss — vomiting, excessive sweating without replacement — even at normal hydration levels.

Overhydration as "flushing toxins": The idea that drinking large volumes of water detoxifies the body is not supported by physiology. The kidneys regulate fluid balance efficiently; pushing well beyond thirst does not accelerate toxin removal and actively dilutes the electrolytes your body needs to function.
