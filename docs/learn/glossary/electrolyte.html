electrolyte — hydration glossary

What it means

An electrolyte is a mineral that carries an electrical charge when dissolved in water. The main electrolytes in the human body are sodium, potassium, magnesium, calcium, chloride, phosphate, and bicarbonate. These charged particles are essential for a wide range of physiological processes, from regulating how much water your cells hold to enabling nerve signals and muscle contractions.

The balance of electrolytes in your blood and tissues is tightly controlled by your kidneys, hormones, and the fluids you drink and lose. When you sweat, urinate, or vomit, you lose electrolytes alongside water — which is why replacing fluids with plain water alone sometimes isn't enough.

Why it matters

Electrolyte balance becomes relevant any time you're losing or shifting large amounts of fluid. Heavy exercise, hot weather, illness involving vomiting or diarrhea, and prolonged fasting can all disrupt electrolyte levels. Low sodium (hyponatremia) is particularly dangerous and can occur when endurance athletes drink excessive plain water without replacing the sodium lost in sweat. Low potassium or magnesium often shows up as muscle cramps, fatigue, or irregular heartbeat. On the clinical side, electrolyte panels are a standard blood test that doctors order to assess kidney function, hydration status, and overall metabolic health.

Example

A runner completes a four-hour marathon in warm conditions, sweating heavily the entire time. She drinks plain water at every aid station but skips the sports drinks. Near the finish line she feels nauseated and confused — early signs of hyponatremia caused by sodium dilution. Had she consumed a sodium-containing drink or taken salt tablets alongside her water, her electrolyte levels would have stayed in a safer range.

Common confusions

Electrolytes vs. minerals — All electrolytes are minerals, but not all minerals function as electrolytes. Iron and zinc are essential minerals that don't carry a meaningful electrical charge in body fluids and are not classified as electrolytes.

Electrolytes vs. hydration — Hydration refers to your total body water status; electrolytes are one component of what keeps that water properly distributed. You can be adequately hydrated in terms of fluid volume yet still have a dangerous electrolyte imbalance, and vice versa.

"Electrolyte drinks" as always necessary — For most people doing light-to-moderate activity lasting under an hour, plain water is sufficient. Electrolyte supplements and sports drinks become genuinely useful mainly during prolonged or intense exercise, illness, or heat exposure — not routine daily drinking.
