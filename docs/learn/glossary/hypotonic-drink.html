hypotonic drink — hydration glossary

What it means

A hypotonic drink contains a lower concentration of dissolved particles (mainly sugars and electrolytes) than blood plasma. Blood sits at roughly 285–295 milliosmoles per kilogram (mOsm/kg); a hypotonic drink comes in below that threshold, typically under 270 mOsm/kg. Because of this concentration difference, the fluid moves relatively quickly across the gut wall and into the bloodstream through osmosis, making it one of the fastest-absorbing drink types.

The sugar content is usually below 4g per 100ml, and electrolytes like sodium and potassium are present in small amounts. Plain water is the simplest example of a hypotonic fluid, though commercial hypotonic drinks add trace electrolytes to support absorption and replace sweat losses.

Why it matters

Hypotonic drinks are most useful when rapid rehydration is the priority and energy replacement is not. They come up in a few specific situations: short-duration exercise where you haven't burned through significant fuel stores, hot environments where sweat rate is high but intensity is low, and everyday rehydration after mild dehydration. Because they absorb quickly, they are also commonly recommended for clinical rehydration in cases of diarrhoea or fever, and oral rehydration sachets are often formulated in the hypotonic range.

Athletes doing longer or higher-intensity sessions typically move away from hypotonic drinks toward isotonic or hypertonic options to meet both fluid and carbohydrate needs simultaneously.

Example

A runner doing an easy 30-minute jog on a warm morning drinks a diluted electrolyte tablet dissolved in 750ml of water. The resulting solution is hypotonic — low in sugar, low in solute concentration — and absorbs into the bloodstream faster than a standard sports drink. Their fluid levels are topped up without adding significant calories, which suits the low-intensity, short-duration effort.

Common confusions

Hypotonic vs. isotonic: Isotonic drinks match the osmolality of blood (roughly 270–330 mOsm/kg). They absorb at a moderate rate and typically contain 4–8g of carbohydrate per 100ml, making them better suited for moderate-to-hard exercise lasting more than an hour. Hypotonic drinks absorb faster but provide little to no fuel.

Hypotonic vs. hypertonic: Hypertonic drinks (energy gels, fruit juice, most soft drinks) have a higher solute concentration than blood. They actually draw water into the gut before absorption occurs, which can cause bloating or slow rehydration if consumed during exercise without additional plain water.

Assuming "dilute" always means hypotonic: Many people dilute sports drinks thinking the result is automatically beneficial. While diluting an isotonic drink can make it hypotonic, the ratio matters — a rough halving of the normal concentration is usually needed to cross that threshold.
