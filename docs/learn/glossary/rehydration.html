rehydration — hydration glossary

What it means

Rehydration is the process of restoring the body's fluid and electrolyte levels after they have been depleted through sweating, illness, exercise, or inadequate intake. It goes beyond simply drinking water — effective rehydration typically involves replacing both fluids and the key electrolytes (sodium, potassium, chloride) lost alongside them. The body's rehydration needs vary depending on how much fluid was lost, how quickly it was lost, and whether losses also included significant electrolytes.

Rehydration can happen through oral intake (drinking fluids or using oral rehydration solutions), through food, or in severe cases, intravenously under medical supervision. The rate at which the body rehydrates depends on factors like the type of fluid consumed, gut absorption speed, and the degree of dehydration.

Why it matters

Rehydration comes up any time fluid losses have outpaced intake. Common scenarios include recovering from a stomach bug with vomiting or diarrhea, returning from a long run or intense workout in the heat, managing a hangover, or helping a child recover from fever-related fluid losses. In clinical settings, proper rehydration is critical for patients who are dehydrated due to surgery, illness, or prolonged fasting.

Getting rehydration right matters because plain water alone can sometimes dilute sodium levels in the blood (particularly after heavy sweating), slowing recovery or causing symptoms like nausea and headache. Using an appropriate fluid — one that includes sodium and other electrolytes — helps the body absorb and retain fluid more efficiently.

Example

A runner finishes a 90-minute summer race and has lost roughly 1.5 liters of sweat. Drinking plain water replaces the volume but not the sodium. By choosing an oral rehydration drink with sodium alongside water and some food, their body absorbs and retains the fluid more effectively, and they recover faster than if they had relied on water alone.

Common confusions

Rehydration vs. hydration: Hydration refers to maintaining adequate fluid balance on an ongoing basis, while rehydration specifically describes the act of correcting a deficit that already exists. You rehydrate after becoming dehydrated; you stay hydrated to prevent it.

Rehydration vs. overhydration: People sometimes assume drinking as much as possible speeds up rehydration, but consuming excessive plain water too quickly can dilute blood sodium and lead to hyponatremia. Rehydration should be paced and ideally involve electrolytes, especially after significant losses.

Oral rehydration solutions (ORS) vs. sports drinks: ORS are specifically formulated for rehydration — they contain precise sodium-to-glucose ratios that optimize intestinal fluid absorption. Sports drinks are designed for hydration during activity and typically have lower sodium and higher sugar, making them less effective for correcting serious fluid deficits.
