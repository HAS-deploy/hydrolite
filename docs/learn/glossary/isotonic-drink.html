isotonic drink — hydration glossary

What it means

An isotonic drink has a concentration of dissolved particles (mainly sugars and electrolytes) that closely matches the concentration found in human blood plasma — roughly 270–330 milliosmoles per kilogram. This balance means the fluid passes from the gut into the bloodstream relatively quickly, delivering both water and carbohydrates at a pace the body can use efficiently. Most commercial sports drinks are formulated to fall within this range, typically containing around 6–8% carbohydrate.

Because the osmolality sits close to that of blood, the body doesn't need to dilute or concentrate the solution before absorbing it. This makes isotonic drinks a practical choice when you need simultaneous fluid replacement and a modest energy supply — unlike plain water, which replaces fluid only, or very sugary drinks, which can slow gastric emptying.

Why it matters

Isotonic drinks come up most often in endurance and team sports contexts, where athletes sweat for 60 minutes or more and need to maintain both hydration and blood glucose. During a football match, a long run, or a cycling event, burning through glycogen while sweating means you need both water and carbohydrate — and an isotonic drink addresses both at once. They also appear in clinical rehydration discussions, though oral rehydration solutions (ORS) used for illness are typically formulated differently and are not the same thing.

Example

A runner competing in a half-marathon drinks 500 ml of a sports drink containing 36 g of carbohydrate per litre during the race. The drink's osmolality sits at around 290 mOsm/kg — matching blood plasma — so it empties from the stomach and absorbs across the gut wall efficiently, providing steady fluid and fuel without causing the bloating or delay that a concentrated juice might.

Common confusions

Hypotonic vs. isotonic: A hypotonic drink has lower osmolality than blood (more dilute than plasma). It absorbs even faster but delivers less carbohydrate per mouthful. Plain water and very dilute electrolyte drinks are effectively hypotonic. People sometimes assume "isotonic" just means "healthy sports drink," but the term is a specific osmolality description, not a quality marker.

Isotonic vs. hypertonic: Hypertonic drinks (fruit juice, cola, concentrated energy drinks) have higher osmolality than blood. They can actually draw water into the gut initially, potentially slowing rehydration — the opposite of what you want mid-exercise.

"Electrolyte water" vs. isotonic: Many electrolyte waters contain trace minerals but almost no carbohydrate, putting their osmolality well below the isotonic range. They are not isotonic drinks in the functional sports-science sense, even if marketed similarly.
